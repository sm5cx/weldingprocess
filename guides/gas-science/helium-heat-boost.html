Helium: Heat Input Boost and Penetration Improvement

Helium's high ionization potential creates a hotter, more energetic arc that increases heat input by 25-40% compared to pure argon, making it essential for thick aluminum and high-thermal-conductivity materials. This extra energy overcomes heat sink effects that would otherwise prevent proper fusion.

How Helium Increases Heat Input

  1. Higher ionization energy required. Helium needs more energy to ionize, creating a hotter arc column.
  2. Increased arc voltage. Same current produces 2-3V higher arc voltage with helium content.
  3. Better heat transfer efficiency. Higher thermal conductivity moves heat into base metal faster.
  4. Wider arc cone. Helium creates broader heat distribution for better fusion on thick materials.

When Helium Additions Pay Off

  • Thick aluminum (>6mm). Overcomes aluminum's high thermal conductivity.
  • Copper and copper alloys. Essential for achieving fusion in high-conductivity metals.
  • High-speed welding. Maintains penetration at faster travel speeds.
  • Out-of-position welding. Higher heat input improves tie-in on vertical and overhead welds.

Helium Percentage Guidelines

25-30% He: Moderate heat increase, good for 6-12mm aluminum, maintains arc stability.

50% He: Significant heat boost for thick sections (12-25mm), industry standard for production.

70-75% He: Maximum practical helium content, for very thick materials or high-speed automation.

Pure helium: Rarely used due to arc instability and gas cost, specialized applications only.
